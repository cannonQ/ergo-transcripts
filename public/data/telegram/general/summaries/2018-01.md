# Ergo General Chat — Monthly Summary: 2018-01

---

## Executive Summary

January 2018 was an early pre-mainnet community-building period for Ergo, dominated by questions about the EFYT-to-ERG token swap mechanics, mining economics, and project timeline. Technical discussions centred on Ergo's foundational design choices — Equihash-based PoW, non-Turing-complete smart contracts via Sigma protocols, and Bitcoin NG-style throughput scaling — as the team worked through TestNet0 and drafted the Yellow Paper.

---

## Key Topics This Month

### EFYT-to-ERG Token Swap
The most consistently recurring topic across all four weeks. A 1:1 swap ratio was confirmed early, with tokens held on the Waves platform requiring no action beyond keeping them in a Waves wallet during conversion. However, the team repeatedly stated that the precise mechanism for rewarding early adopters beyond the base 1:1 ratio remained undefined, and was expected to be clarified as TestNet progressed. Community members asked repeatedly whether a premium rate or bonus structure would apply; no concrete answer was given by month's end.

### Token Supply and Mining Economics
Circulating EFYT supply stood at approximately 400k tokens against a total of 5 million EFYT. Post-mainnet, total ERG supply was stated as 100 million, with approximately 50 million tokens estimated to be mined in the first year depending on miner participation. The team fee was set at 10% of mined tokens. These figures were consistently flagged as rough estimates, and the long-term emission schedule and halving curve were not detailed during this month.

### Proof-of-Work Design and Mining Accessibility
Ergo's Equihash-based PoW was explained as a deliberate choice to democratise mining — enabling light-full nodes and even mobile devices to mine profitably, in contrast to ASIC-dominated chains. Bitcoin NG protocol was cited as a planned first-layer scaling approach, with theoretical throughput up to 1,100 TPS; Waves was referenced as having achieved 100 TPS in early implementation. PoW was defended against emerging PoS alternatives on the grounds of security and decentralisation.

### Non-Turing-Complete Smart Contracts and Sigma Protocols
Community member @hawky provided detailed explanations of why Ergo's non-Turing-complete approach to smart contracts reduces attack surface compared to Ethereum's Turing-complete model, noting that loops and recursion in Turing-complete languages have historically enabled exploits. Preliminary research was cited suggesting approximately 75% of Ethereum contracts could be implemented without Turing completeness. The Sigma protocol paper was released during the month to community enthusiasm, with acknowledgment that the technical depth would require careful study. Privacy features via Sigma were confirmed as planned post-implementation.

### Development Status and Timeline
TestNet0 version 0.2.0 was released during Week 2. The Yellow Paper was described as under active editing. The team had two full-time developers publicly announced, with five more to be announced shortly. Mainnet launch was described as requiring more than three months from mid-January, with a website and developer update expected in February. A Q&A session was planned to address outstanding community questions about supply, airdrops, and project details.

### Token Storage and Security
Multiple community members asked about safe custody of EFYT on the Waves platform. The consensus was that the Waves Lite Client (desktop wallet) is secure provided machine security is maintained, and is analogous to a non-custodial wallet rather than a centralised exchange. Cold storage support for Waves was expected by February. Paper wallet generation was noted as an option.

## Technical Q&A Worth Preserving

- **Q** (@Unknown, 2018-01-05): What are the implications of Hashgraph consensus for Ergo?
  **A** (@mentalist420): Ergo is PoW-based using Equihash. The Bitcoin NG protocol is planned as a first-layer implementation, theoretically yielding 1,100 TPS. Waves already demonstrated 100 TPS with an initial NG implementation and will adjust configuration for higher throughput.

- **Q** (@Unknown, 2018-01-05): Will Ergo have private transactions?
  **A** (@hawky): "It will, implemented via non-Turing complete smart contracts." Monero-like privacy will be included right after Sigma implementation; more complicated schemes may follow.

- **Q** (@Rejinderi, 2018-01-05): Will mainnet launch and mining cause token price to drop?
  **A** (@mentalist420): Historical precedent (ZEC) shows an initial spike then stabilisation. Value is likely to rise post-mainnet because: (1) no ICO is planned — funding is via token issuance only; (2) approximately 50 million tokens mined over the first year depending on miner participation; (3) the swap mechanism is designed to reward early adopters.

- **Q** (@Rejinderi, 2018-01-05): How will the token swap to ERG be executed?
  **A** (@mentalist420): "Storing on Waves wallet is enough; the swap will be a simple procedure — just a transfer of assets to another account and you'll receive ERG on a new wallet to be provided."

- **Q** (@Unknown, 2018-01-14): Why is Ergo using PoW when most projects move to PoS?
  **A** (@mentalist420): Ergo's Equihash-based PoW aims to decentralise mining by allowing light-full node operation without downloading the full chain, removing the hardware barriers that dominate other PoW systems.

- **Q** (@gd1551, 2018-01-15): What do you mean by "safe smart contracts"?
  **A** (@hawky): Ethereum uses a Turing-complete language enabling loops and recursion, which have caused hacks (e.g., the DAO). Non-Turing-complete languages prevent these error classes but cannot implement every function. Preliminary research suggests approximately 75% of Ethereum contracts could work without Turing completeness.

- **Q** (@Unknown, 2018-01-23): Is Ergo a competitor to Waves since both introduce non-Turing smart contracts?
  **A** (@mentalist420): Both are pieces of the same puzzle. Each platform offers distinct solutions and neither is a direct competitor — blockchain is complex enough to need complementary approaches.

- **Q** (@Unknown, 2018-01-23): Where can EFYT be held safely? Is there a cold wallet option?
  **A** (@mentalist420): Tokens can be held on waveswallet.io. Cold storage support is expected by February. The desktop Waves Lite Client functions as a secure wallet if machine security is maintained.

- **Q** (@Unknown, 2018-01-24): Is the Waves Lite Client safe, or does it face DNS-hack risks like EtherDelta?
  **A** (@Waylay): Waves is decentralised infrastructure analogous to MyEtherWallet for Ethereum — not a centralised exchange. Centralised exchanges face those attack vectors; non-custodial peer-to-peer clients do not.
