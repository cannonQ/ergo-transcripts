# Ergo General — Monthly Developer Knowledge Base Summary
**Chat:** Ergo General
**Month:** 2018-05
**Weeks covered:** W18, W20, W22

---

## Executive Summary

May 2018 was a foundational month for Ergo, dominated by protocol design rationale, tokenomics clarification, and the successful launch of TestNet1 on 2018-05-31. Community discussion centred on three core themes: establishing Ergo's independence from Cardano/IOHK, explaining the economic design (emission curve, EFYT swap mechanics, post-emission incentives), and articulating the technical philosophy around ASIC resistance, block time, and stateless clients.

---

## Key Topics This Month

### Ergo's Independence from Cardano / IOHK

A recurring clarification throughout the month: while Ergo's founders (notably kushti/Chepurnoy and Dmitry Meshkov) collaborated with IOHK on academic research (NIPoPoWs and related work), Ergo shares no codebase with Cardano and was built entirely from scratch using the Scorex framework. Dmitry explicitly noted he had been removed from IOHK's website to prevent ongoing confusion. Ergo's use of Scorex predates IOHK involvement — kushti created the first Scorex version in 2015 before joining IOHK's research team. The Waves connection similarly derives from Waves' early adoption of an older Scorex version, not from any organisational relationship.

### EFYT Token, Emission Curve & Swap Mechanics

The EFYT token (issued on Waves DEX) represents a futures claim against 10% of first-year Ergo mining output (~1.971M tokens from the team allocation). The planned mainnet swap is 1:1, with EFYT holders receiving tokens on both the Σ-Data and Σ-Cash chains (effectively 2 tokens per 1 EFYT). The emission curve was published at `ergoplatform.org/news_emission_curve.html` and features steep upward issuance approximately doubling annually until an inflection point around years 4–5, with full emission at ~8 years. Community members raised substantive concerns about whether an 8-year emission window is sufficient for long-term network security compared to Bitcoin, and what concrete mechanisms will incentivise miners and node operators after mining concludes. These questions remained formally unresolved entering June.

### ASIC Resistance & Mining Philosophy

The community discussed Bitmain's Z9 Equihash ASIC announcement and the centralisation risk it represented. Ergo's position was that frequent hard-fork anti-ASIC strategies (as employed by Monero) contradict the protocol's core design principle of simplicity — hard forks would only be warranted for critical pre-mainnet issues. ASIC resistance is a major design objective but pursued through Autolykos's memory-hardness rather than reactive hard-forks. A testnet node was demonstrated running on solar power as an illustration of energy-efficiency potential. CPU, GPU, and FPGA mining were all discussed as viable approaches under Ergo's model.

### Block Time Design (2-Minute Interval)

The 2-minute block time was explained as a deliberate choice to minimise chain reorganisations and orphan blocks while remaining fast enough for network propagation. Shorter intervals risk consensus failure, particularly on smaller or slower-growing networks. Importantly, the planned future implementation of Bitcoin-NG (NG) was identified as the mechanism that would enable sub-second transaction confirmation without requiring a change to the base block interval — distinguishing confirmation latency from block production rate. Contracts and dApps that rely on block height rather than timestamps were noted as a consideration requiring careful design.

### Testnet Roadmap

TestNet1 launched on schedule on 2018-05-31, marking the first public deployment of the Sigma-State scripting language with oracle examples. TestNet2 was announced for September 2018, designed as a mainnet prototype incorporating finalised proof of work parameters. Mainnet launch was targeted for winter 2018. Minimum node requirements at time of writing were 3–4 GB RAM, with updated specifications expected alongside the TestNet1 release. Cross-chain atomic swaps between Waves and Ergo testnets were flagged as a planned capability leveraging contract support on both chains.

### Academic Research & Stateless Clients

Ergo's foundation in novel academic research was emphasised repeatedly. Key research directions highlighted included stateless clients, NIPoPoWs (enabling mobile and lightweight mining), and new economic models for blockchain sustainability. A University of Maryland grant for blockchain security research (covering stateless client work) was shared as evidence of external academic validation. kushti's part-time research role at IOHK and participation in Zcash grants and blockchain security commissions were noted as ongoing context.

## Technical Q&A Worth Preserving

- **Q** (@Unknown, 2018-05-03): Is the Ergo protocol built from scratch or derived from Cardano?
  **A** (@𝖒𝖊𝖓𝖙𝖆𝖑𝖎𝖘𝖙կշօ): Built entirely from scratch using the Scorex framework. See `github.com/ScorexFoundation/Scorex` and `github.com/ergoplatform/ergo/releases`. Cardano is written in Haskell and does not use Scorex.

- **Q** (@Unknown, 2018-05-18): Why was Waves chosen for EFYT distribution rather than another platform?
  **A** (@Unknown): No organisational relationship exists with Waves developers. Waves adopted an early Scorex version and built asset-to-asset exchange with instant matching as native transaction types — providing a non-custodial, secure, fast DEX without requiring external exchange listings before mainnet.

- **Q** (@Unknown, 2018-05-22): Why was 2 minutes chosen as the block interval?
  **A** (@Unknown): 2 minutes minimises chain reorganisations and orphan blocks while remaining fast enough for network propagation. Shorter intervals risk consensus failure. Future Bitcoin-NG implementation will enable few-second transaction confirmation without changing the block time.

- **Q** (@Unknown, 2018-05-22): Will Ergo use proof of stake for oracles?
  **A** (@Unknown): Ergo uses proof of work. TestNet1 includes Sigma-State with oracle examples; full specifications available before release.

- **Q** (@C S, 2018-05-28): What is the EFYT token and how does the mainnet swap work?
  **A** (@Unknown): EFYT represents a futures claim on 10% of first-year Ergo mining output (~1.971M tokens from team allocation). Mainnet swap is 1:1, with holders receiving tokens on both Σ-Data and Σ-Cash chains — effectively 2 tokens per 1 EFYT, similar in structure to Zcash's approach but fully public.

- **Q** (@C S, 2018-05-28): What incentives exist for miners and node operators after the full ERG supply is emitted at ~year 8?
  **A** (@Unknown): Incentive structures post-emission are critical and still under detailed review. Community governance (including potential founder removal by vote) is part of the long-term design. No concrete mechanism was specified — this remains an open design question.

- **Q** (@Unknown, 2018-05-28): Is Ergo implementing sharding, and will it be available to other projects?
  **A** (@Unknown): Ergo plans both implementation and provision of sharding/stateless client solutions. Adoption by other projects depends on their willingness and technical capacity.
